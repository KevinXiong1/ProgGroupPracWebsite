In 1988, The TAT-8 Cable was laid along the sea bed of the Atlantic Ocean, and was the first active use of fibre-optic technology. It allowed for any binary message to be sent across the Atlantic Ocean at a speed of 35MB/s. This allowed for near-instantaneous communication between continents.

This cable would then spark a light for a number of new technologies:

International Communications & Telephone Calls

  • As said before, you could send any message you wanted at a very high speed.
  • You could now talk to your relatives across the world as if they were in the same country as you! (Just at a higher price, depending on the provider)

The Development of the World Wide Web

  • IBM created a link between Cornell University and CERN using the TAT-8 cable.
  • This allowed Tim Berners-Lee a high-speed connection to the National Science Foundation Network in America, which aided the development of the World Wide Web immensely.
  • It also lead to the acceptance of the TCP/IP protocol in Europe.
(Source)

Usage of Fibre-Optic Worldwide

  • Ever since the first submarine cables, the production of fibre-optic cables has been more cost-efficient than using the old copper cables.
  • This lead to Internet Providers around the world installing fibre-optic cabling not only under water, but on land as well.
  • As such, we are able to take full advantage of fibre-optic speeds without many bottlenecks in the form of copper cabling.
